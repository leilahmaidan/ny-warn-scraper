New York State Felt The Hit of April’s Jobs Report

The wide-spread devastation caused by the global pandemic and lockdowns brought the U.S. economy to its knees in April, as the unemployment rate surged to a record-breaking 14.7% and, wiped out the last 20 years of job gains. New York State was not spared as it took a high hit of losses in the restaurant and hotel sector.

The monthly report, released by The Labor Department, reflected the widespread impact of the virus as the economy took away 20.5 million jobs in one month. Low-wage workers made up the brunt of the losses. The percentage of the adult population with a job now stands at an unprecedented 51.3%.

By Laila Maiden
Published May 25, 2020 8:00 PM

The state of New York saw 497 restaurants and 144 hotels impacted; with 72 % of hotels and 71% of restaurants being from New York County alone.

“This is going to get worse before it gets better,” said Joe Brusuelas, chief economist at RSM US. “We have only really captured a glimpse of what the public is absorbing from the shock.,”

These numbers do not even include the number of people who are still employed in the sectors but have had their hours slashed substantially. Since the lockdowns began, some restaurants remined open for takeout only and under limited hours. Employees that once worked full days now work less hours, while tips for in-house dinning have been diminished with takeout.

As for hotels, the few chains that were able to remain open were ones that secured government contracts and private sector contracts related to putting up healthcare workers who had come into the state to fight COVID-19. The rest were forced to all but shut down due to the lack of travelers.

Economists’ worry that some of the losses may become permanent as businesses struggle to recover while others shut down for good. Even when the economy starts to slowly re-open, most businesses won’t be able to operate at full capacity. Restaurants and shops may need to limit the number of customers they take in, while consumers may be afraid to go out or spend money. While it may take people some time before they are comfortable traveling again, leaving hotels struggling to fill rooms even after the lockdowns end.

Though not all economists are looking at it negatively.

“The data represents the rear-view mirror,” said Thomas Hayes, Chairman of Great Hill Capital. “… People are underestimating the impact that all of the stimulus will have by [the fourth quarter of] 2020 and [the first half of] 2021. It'll be bumpy in the interim with more rear-view looking ‘bad news’,’ but the worst is behind us.,”

Other economists believe the recovery will not depend on any economic factors such as a re-opening or a stimulus package, but a vaccine. “If the pandemic is not addressed by the medical and scientific communities, that is, if there isn’t a vaccine [found], we have longer periods of low economic growth or contraction with elevated rates,” said Brusuelas
